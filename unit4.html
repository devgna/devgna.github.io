Unit 4

1. System Design Levels

  1. Architectural Design:
    • Abstract view of the system.
    • Focus on components and their interactions.
    • Establishes the overall structure of the solution domain.
  2. High-Level Design:
    • Breaks down the architecture into subsystems and modules.
    • Defines modular relationships and interactions.
    • Bridges the gap between architecture and detailed implementation.
  3. Detailed Design:
    • Focuses on module-level implementation.
    • Specifies data flows, logic, and interfaces.
    • Enables coding and testing at the granular level.

2. Design of Output System

  1. Definition:
    • Outputs are information/results generated by the system.
  2. Key Objectives:
    • Provide insights on status, actions, or projections.
    • Highlight alerts, warnings, or opportunities.
    • Trigger actions or confirm user operations.
  3. Steps in Output Design:
    • Identify what information to present.
    • Choose the output medium (e.g., screen, print, voice).
    • Arrange data into a clear layout (headings, columns).
    • Define the distribution method for recipients.
  4. Output Types:
    • Reports: Detailed summaries or analytical insights.
    • Notifications: Alerts and messages.
    • Documents: Help manuals, receipts.
  5. Presentation Formats:
    • Tabular, graphical, icons, or color-coded displays.

3. Form Design

  1. Definition:
    • A form is a data entry interface for capturing system inputs.
  2. Key Considerations:
    • Layout: Logical arrangement of fields and sections.
    • Captions: Clear prompts for data input.
    • Coding Methods: Unique identifiers, efficient storage.
    • Data Capture Tools: Keyboards, scanners, or punch cards.
  3. Sections of a Form:
    • Heading: Title of the form.
    • Identification: Details for access or tracking.
    • Instructions: Guidelines for completion.
    • Body: Core fields for data entry.
    • Verification: Signature or confirmation section.
    • Comments: Space for notes or additional input.
  4. Good Design Principles:
    • Ease of Use: Intuitive and simple.
    • Accuracy: Minimize errors with clear instructions.
    • Attractiveness: Clean and uncluttered layout with appropriate visuals.
    • Use fonts, colors, and prompts to guide users effectively.
