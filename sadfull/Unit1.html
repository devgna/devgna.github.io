UNIT -1 INTRODUCTION TO SYSTEM DEVELOPMENT

1) Characteristics of a System

  • Organization: Structure and order to achieve objectives. For example, a computer system organizes hardware and software to function smoothly.
  • Interaction: Refers to how components of the system work together. For example, how the keyboard inputs data and the screen displays the output.
  • Interdependence: The parts of the system depend on each other. If one component fails, the entire system may be affected. For example, in a business, sales depend on marketing, and marketing depends on product development.
  • Integration: All parts of the system work seamlessly together. For example, in a car, the engine, brakes, and steering system are integrated to ensure safe operation.
  • Central Objective: A system should have a clear goal. For example, a payroll system’s goal is to calculate employee salaries.

2) Elements of a System

  • Outputs and Inputs: Inputs are data or information that enter a system, and outputs are the results after processing.
  • Processor(s): The component that processes inputs into outputs.
  • Control: This component makes decisions regarding the flow of inputs, processing, and outputs.
  • Feedback: Feedback is used to maintain system balance, offering positive or negative responses based on system performance.
  • Environment: External elements (e.g., vendors, competitors) affect the system’s performance.
  • Boundaries and Interface: Boundaries define the limits of the system, and interfaces describe how a system interacts with other systems or components.

3) System Analyst

  • Role: The system analyst helps design and develop systems that meet organizational needs. They understand the requirements of users and guide system development.
    • Main Roles: Defining user needs, prioritizing requirements, suggesting solutions, and implementing modular system designs.
    • Attributes:
      • Interpersonal Skills: Ability to work with users and programmers, manage expectations, and lead teams.
      • Analytical Skills: Ability to study systems, identify problems, and solve them logically.
      • Management Skills: Ability to manage resources and handle change/risk management.
      • Technical Skills: Knowledge of modern technologies, system design tools, and software.

4) Application Development Portfolio (ADP)

  • Definition: The set of proposed or ongoing system development projects within an organization. The goal is to solve problems, capitalize on opportunities, or meet directives.
    • Examples:
      • Solving problems (e.g., automating data entry).
      • Capitalizing on opportunities (e.g., offering customer discounts).
      • Responding to directives (e.g., complying with legal requirements).

5) The 5 C’s

  • Capability: Speed, volume, and retrieval of data.
  • Control: Accuracy, consistency, and security in data handling.
  • Communication: Facilitating fast information flow and integrating business operations.
  • Cost: Monitoring and reducing costs, ensuring efficient resource usage.
  • Competitiveness: Locking in customers and suppliers, enhancing product development, and maintaining an edge over competitors.

6) Information Systems

  • Computer-Based Information Systems (CBIS): These systems use computer technology to manage business processes and information. They ensure timely and accurate decision-making by integrating hardware, software, and human elements.
    • Types of Information Systems:
      • Transaction Processing System (TPS): Manages business transactions.
      • Management Information System (MIS): Summarizes data for middle management.
      • Decision Support System (DSS): Assists decision-making in semi-structured situations.
      • Expert Systems: Use artificial intelligence to aid in problem-solving.
